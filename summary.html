Language Model Assignment Summary

This report summarises all experiments performed during the assignment, the final architecture, observations from every training run, and the reasoning behind selecting the final submitted checkpoint. The objective throughout the project was to minimise development Bits-Per-Byte (BPB) while remaining below the 2 million parameter limit.

Project Objective

The starter implementation consisted of a small decoder-only Transformer trained on a byte-level tokenizer. The goal of the assignment was to improve language modelling performance through architectural changes, optimisation improvements and better training strategies while respecting the parameter budget and training constraints.

Rather than applying arbitrary modifications, every experiment was treated as a hypothesis. After each training run the development BPB was analysed, the changes were evaluated and only successful modifications were retained. This report therefore documents both successful and unsuccessful experiments.

Technical Discussion

Run 1

The first experiment focused on improving both optimisation and model capacity. Increasing the number of transformer layers, embedding dimension and context length significantly increased representational power while remaining below the parameter budget. Enabling weight tying reduced parameter redundancy by sharing the embedding matrix and output projection. The additional optimisation improvements, particularly cosine learning-rate decay and gradient clipping, resulted in stable optimisation throughout training. Overall this experiment established a substantially stronger baseline.

Run 2

The second experiment produced the best result. Doubling the batch size reduced stochastic gradient noise, producing smoother optimisation and consistently lower training loss. Increasing warmup to 200 steps prevented excessively large updates during the earliest training iterations, improving optimisation stability. Introducing a small dropout probability of 0.05 improved generalisation without noticeably slowing convergence. This experiment simultaneously reduced training loss and development BPB, indicating improvements in both optimisation quality and model generalisation.

Run 3

The third experiment investigated architectural modifications inspired by modern GPT implementations. Despite their popularity in large language models, GPT-style initialisation, bias removal and approximate GELU collectively reduced performance. The most likely explanation is that these techniques are beneficial for very large models trained on billions of tokens, whereas the assignment model is comparatively small and trained for only 2000 optimisation steps. Removing attention biases also reduces model flexibility during the early stages of optimisation, making learning slightly more difficult. This experiment demonstrated that techniques successful in production-scale models do not necessarily improve performance in small training regimes.

Run 4

The final experiment attempted to improve modelling capacity further by increasing the context length from 256 to 384 while simultaneously reducing the learning rate. Although longer contexts are generally desirable, they substantially increased the optimisation difficulty under the fixed budget of 2000 optimisation steps. At the same time, lowering the learning rate reduced optimisation speed. Consequently, the model failed to converge as effectively as Run 2, resulting in both higher training loss and significantly worse development BPB. This illustrates an important optimisation principle: increasing model capability alone is insufficient unless adequate optimisation resources are also available.

Why Run 2 Was Selected

Run 2 achieved the lowest development BPB of 2.5578, making it the best-performing model across all experiments. Although subsequent experiments explored more sophisticated architectural ideas, none produced lower BPB. The final checkpoint therefore corresponds to the Run 2 configuration because it provided the strongest balance between optimisation quality, generalisation performance and computational efficiency. The experiments also highlighted that careful optimisation improvements (batch size, warmup and moderate dropout) provided larger gains than adopting architectural techniques intended for substantially larger language models.

Human vs Machine Contribution

The overall experimental process was driven by human decision making. The hypotheses for each experiment, interpretation of the training curves, selection of subsequent experiments and final evaluation of successful and unsuccessful modifications were all performed manually. An AI coding assistant was used to accelerate implementation by suggesting code modifications, explaining optimisation techniques and assisting in debugging implementation issues. The AI was also used to discuss possible architectural alternatives and explain expected optimisation behaviour. All experiments were independently executed by the author, with every proposed change being validated through actual training and development evaluation. Several AI-suggested modifications were rejected after empirical evaluation because they degraded development BPB, demonstrating that final decisions were based on experimental evidence rather than accepting generated code without verification. Overall, approximately 60–70% of the implementation effort (code generation, debugging assistance and documentation drafting) was assisted by AI, while 30–40% of the work (experimental design, evaluation, interpretation of results, comparison of runs and final model selection) was performed through human judgement.

Final Remarks

This project demonstrated that optimisation strategy was considerably more important than introducing increasingly complex architectural modifications. The largest improvements were obtained through increased model capacity, weight tying, improved optimisation, larger batch size, longer warmup and light regularisation. Subsequent experiments further reinforced the importance of empirical evaluation by showing that several theoretically appealing modifications actually reduced performance under the assignment constraints. The final submitted checkpoint therefore represents the best experimentally validated model obtained during the project.
